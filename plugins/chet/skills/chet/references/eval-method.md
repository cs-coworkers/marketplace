# Evaluating Claude's output

You can't improve what you can't judge. The difference between a prompt that "seems fine" and one you trust is having a clear way to tell good output from bad — and checking against it instead of going on impression.

**Define "good" before you judge.** Finish the sentence "this output is good if ___" — accurate, in the right format, covers these points, this tone, this length. That standard is your yardstick; without it you're reacting to vibes, and vibes drift.

**Check against the test, not the gloss.** Fluent, confident writing is easy to mistake for correct writing. Read the output against your criteria point by point — especially any facts, numbers, names, or claims, which deserve a direct spot-check against a source rather than trust.

**Test on real, representative cases.** A prompt that works on the easy demo example often breaks on the messy real ones. Try it on a few genuine inputs, including the hard and edge cases, before you rely on it.

**Compare on equal footing.** When choosing between two prompts (or two models), run them on the *same* inputs and compare the results side by side. Changing several things at once tells you nothing — change one thing at a time so you know what actually moved the result.

**Iterate deliberately.** Improvement is a loop: judge, adjust one thing, re-run, judge again. Two or three focused passes beat one big rewrite, because each pass teaches you what the last change did.

**For anything repeated or automated, keep a small test set.** If you'll run a prompt many times — or hand it to an automated workflow — write down a handful of fixed example inputs with what a good answer looks like. Re-run them whenever you change the prompt or switch models. It's a cheap early warning that something quietly got worse, which is exactly the failure you won't notice by eye.

**Decide what "good enough" is.** Perfect is expensive. Set the bar the task actually needs, confirm the output clears it, and stop there.
